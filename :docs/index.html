Neuro-Symbolic AI for Drug Safety

Mechanistically grounding off-target toxicity signals in biology

Case Study: Pralsetinib

The Challenge

Modern pharmacovigilance systems detect statistical safety signals, but rarely explain why adverse events occur.

When a patient develops infection, hepatic injury, or neurological symptoms, what biological mechanisms connect those outcomes to molecular drug interactions?

In healthcare, predictive accuracy is not enough. Interpretability is essential.

Research Question

Can ontologies ground AI models for drug toxicity analysis by enabling mechanistically interpretable reasoning from molecular interactions to real-world adverse events?

Our Approach

We constructed a multi-layer knowledge graph integrating curated biological databases with real-world post-marketing safety data.

Drug
↓
Protein Interaction
↓
Biological Process (GO)
↓
Toxicity Theme

This structure enables path-based reasoning across molecular, biological, and clinical layers.

Data Integration

DrugBank

Defines high-confidence drug–protein interactions.

Gene Ontology

Maps proteins to biological processes for mechanistic reasoning.

FAERS

Provides real-world adverse event signals used for weak supervision.

Interactive Knowledge Graph

Explore how Pralsetinib connects to proteins, biological processes, and observed toxicity themes.

Modeling Strategy

Baseline: FAERS-Only Ranking

Toxicity themes ranked purely by adverse event frequency. No biological reasoning incorporated.

Ontology-Grounded Path Scoring

Mechanistic paths of the form:

Drug → Protein → GO Process → Toxicity Theme

Paths are weighted by biological role and FAERS support, enabling interpretable hypothesis generation.

Technical Architecture

Nodes include Drug, Protein, GO Biological Process, and Toxicity Theme. Edges encode molecular binding, pathway involvement, and real-world safety associations. Modeling includes path-based scoring and optional feature extraction for interpretable ML models.

Observations

  • Ontology-grounded scoring produces biologically coherent hypotheses.
  • Mechanistic chains link immune signaling pathways to infection reports.
  • Structured reasoning improves interpretability over frequency-based ranking.

Why This Matters

Most AI systems in healthcare produce probabilities. This framework produces mechanistic explanations.

Ontology-grounded reasoning bridges molecular biology and patient-level outcomes, improving transparency in drug safety analysis.

Limitations

  • Single-drug case study
  • Manual components in GO-to-toxicity alignment
  • Reporting bias in FAERS data
